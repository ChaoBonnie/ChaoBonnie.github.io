Who am I?

About me

I am a biomedical engineer interested in bringing data-driven solutions to clinical diagnostics and decision-making. Machine learning is a relatively new application in the field of medicine, and I believe there is a lot of potential for its use in clinical practice to improve healthcare workflows and patient quality of life.

Currently, I'm a PhD candidate at University of Toronto, under the supervision of Dr. Shaf Keshavjee and Dr. Bo Wang. My research lies on the intersection between data science and lung transplantation, employing advanced machine learning techniques and leveraging extensive donor lung data. Our goal is to predict transplantation outcomes, thereby assisting surgeons in making informed decisions regarding donor lung suitability for transplant. Additionally, I actively mentor junior students both inside and outside of the academic sphere through teaching assistant roles and various mentorship programs.

In parallel with my academic pursuits, I've gained diverse professional experience in customer service within the cosmetic industry and process engineering in semiconductor research. The exposure to individuals from diverse backgrounds has allowed me to develop strong interpersonal skills and a global perspective.

Outside of work, I enjoy playing piano, hiking, and skiing. I am also committed to enhancing my French proficiency and bringing it to a professional level. In the end, learning new knowledge, meeting new people, and exploring new perspectives are things that keep my personal and professional journey exciting!

Education

Education

PhD - Biomedical Engineering 2019-present
University of Toronto

I am currently pursuing a PhD under the supervision of professors Shaf Keshavjee and Bo Wang, where my main research focuses on the development of AI models to diagnose donor lungs outside of the body and predict transplant outcomes. The types of data analyzed include patient COVID-19 emergency department data, as well as ex vivo lung tabular and radiographic data (N=100-1,500). Traditional machine learning and deep learning methods were applied, such as regression, Extreme Gradient Boosting, and convolutional neural networks.

Additionally, the reliability of lung sampling across different locations was examined through extensive statistical analyses. This study uncovered compelling evidence pertinent to an ongoing debate in thoracic surgery, leading to consequential changes in both clinical and research practices in lung sampling at Toronto General Hospital.

The research work so far has resulted in four first-author manuscripts (including one article featured as a journal cover), four co-author manuscripts, two patent applications, more than five mentored students, and three awards. A list of publications can be found in the research section below.

BASc - Materials Engineering 2014-2019
University of Toronto

My curriculum was broadly focused on material characteristics and physical chemistry. I also took a number of biomaterial courses and obtained a minor in bioengineering. I completed a one-year process engineering internship at Air Liquide, which is described in the experience section below.

I did my final-year thesis project with Naomi Matsuura. My teammate and I synthesized and characterized perfluorooctyl bromide nano-droplets as a drug carrier, and then investigated the drug release profile through peroxidation of the lipid membrane.
